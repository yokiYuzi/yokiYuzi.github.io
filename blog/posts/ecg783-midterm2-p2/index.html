ECG 783 Midterm #2 — Problem 2: IIR LMS Adaptive Inverse System

2026-04-27  ·  ECE · Adaptive Signal Processing

1. Problem Statement

An unknown plant $H_p(z)$ produces an output $u(k)$ from a white-noise input $s(k)$. The goal is to train an adaptive IIR filter $H(z)$ — placed in series with the plant — so that the cascade $H(z)\,H_p(z) \to 1$, i.e.\ the filter learns the exact inverse of the plant. The desired signal is $d(k) = s(k)$ (delay $\Delta = 0$), and the error is $\varepsilon(k) = d(k) - y(k)$.

s(k) ──► [ H_p(z) ] ──► u(k) ──► [ H(z) ] ──► y(k) │ │ └──────────── d(k) = s(k) ─── Σ ◄─────────────┘ │ ε(k) = d(k) − y(k)

The plant and adaptive filter transfer functions are:

$$H_p(z) = \frac{1 + 1.3\,z^{-1} + 0.8\,z^{-2}}{1 - 1.2\,z^{-1} + 0.6\,z^{-2}}$$ $$H(z) = \frac{a_0 + a_1 z^{-1} + a_2 z^{-2}}{1 - b_1 z^{-1} - b_2 z^{-2}}$$

The weight vector is $\mathbf{w} = [a_0,\,a_1,\,a_2,\,b_1,\,b_2]^T$ (5 parameters total).

2. Optimal Solution

For $H(z)\,H_p(z) = 1$ the adaptive filter must equal the exact inverse of the plant:

$$H_\text{opt}(z) = \frac{1}{H_p(z)} = \frac{1 - 1.2\,z^{-1} + 0.6\,z^{-2}}{1 + 1.3\,z^{-1} + 0.8\,z^{-2}}$$

Matching coefficients against the parameterisation of $H(z)$:

  • Numerator: $a_0 = 1,\quad a_1 = -1.2,\quad a_2 = 0.6$
  • Denominator: $1 - b_1 z^{-1} - b_2 z^{-2} = 1 + 1.3 z^{-1} + 0.8 z^{-2}$ $\;\Rightarrow\; b_1 = -1.3,\;\; b_2 = -0.8$
$$\mathbf{w}_\text{opt} = \begin{bmatrix}1 & -1.2 & 0.6 & -1.3 & -0.8\end{bmatrix}^T$$

3. IIR LMS Update Equations

Because the filter is recursive, the standard LMS gradient is approximated by a pseudo-gradient $\boldsymbol{\psi}(k)$. The update rule is:

$$\mathbf{w}(k+1) = \mathbf{w}(k) + 2\,\mathbf{M}\,\boldsymbol{\psi}(k)\,\varepsilon(k)$$

where $\mathbf{M} = \mu\,\mathbf{I}$ is the step-size matrix.

Gradient vector $\boldsymbol{\psi}(k)$

The pseudo-gradient is computed by filtering the regressor signals through $H(z)$ itself:

$$\boldsymbol{\psi}(k) = \frac{\partial y(k)}{\partial \mathbf{w}} = \begin{bmatrix} \phi_{u}(k) \\ \phi_{u}(k-1) \\ \phi_{u}(k-2) \\ \phi_{y}(k-1) \\ \phi_{y}(k-2) \end{bmatrix}$$

where $\phi_u(k)$ is $u(k)$ filtered through $H(z)$, and $\phi_y(k)$ are the recursively filtered output terms.

5. Average Learning Curve

Figure 2 shows the learning curve averaged over 50 independent trials (dB scale). The MSE decays exponentially toward a near-zero floor, confirming stable convergence of the IIR LMS algorithm with $\mu = 0.001$.

The MSE time constant $T_\text{MSE}$ is related to the step size by $T_\text{MSE} \approx \frac{1}{4\,\mu\,\lambda_\text{avg}}$, where $\lambda_\text{avg}$ is the average eigenvalue of the input correlation matrix. For white noise with unit variance the eigenvalues are all unity, giving $T_\text{MSE} \approx \frac{1}{4 \times 0.001} = 250$, though the recursive structure of the IIR filter effectively increases the time constant relative to an FIR of the same order.

6. Frequency Response Verification

Figure 3 compares the magnitude and phase responses of the plant $H_p(z)$, the converged adaptive inverse $H(z)$, and their cascade $H(z)\,H_p(z)$. A flat 0 dB magnitude and 0° phase in the cascade confirms that the filter has successfully learned the exact inverse.

7. Conclusion

The IIR LMS algorithm with step-size matrix $\mathbf{M} = 0.001\,\mathbf{I}$ successfully identifies the inverse of the third-order IIR plant $H_p(z)$ using a 5-parameter adaptive IIR filter. Key observations:

  • All five weights converge to the theoretical optimum $\mathbf{w}_\text{opt} = [1,\,-1.2,\,0.6,\,-1.3,\,-0.8]^T$ within roughly $3\text{–}4 \times 10^3$ iterations.
  • The average learning curve (50 trials) reaches an MSE floor near $-156\,\text{dB}$, indicating numerical-precision-level residual error.
  • The frequency-response comparison confirms $H(z)\,H_p(z) \approx 1$ at all frequencies — both magnitude flat at $0\,\text{dB}$ and phase identically $0°$.
  • Unlike FIR inverse approximations, the IIR structure achieves an exact inverse with only 3 numerator and 2 denominator taps, matching the plant order precisely.
