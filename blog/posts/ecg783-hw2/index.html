ECG 783 HW2 — FIR Identification of an IIR System via Wiener Solution

Course: ECG 783  ·  Student: Chang Wang (2002721058)  ·  Instructor: Peter Stubberud  ·  02-22-2026

1. Objective

Identify an unknown discrete-time IIR system using an Adaptive Linear Combiner (ALC) with $L+1$ adjustable FIR weights. The unknown system is:

$$d(k) = 0.5\,d(k-1) + 32\,x(k) + 32\,x(k-1),$$

where $x(k)$ is the input and $d(k)$ is the desired output. Two filter lengths are studied: $L+1=2$ and $L+1=10$. For each case, $R$, $P$, and the Wiener solution $W=R\backslash P$ are computed; time-domain signals and frequency responses are compared.

2. System and Model Description

2.1 Unknown IIR system

In $z^{-1}$ notation the transfer function is:

$$H(z) = \frac{D(z)}{X(z)} = \frac{32 + 32z^{-1}}{1 - 0.5z^{-1}}.$$

This is an IIR system due to the feedback pole at $z=0.5$. Its impulse response decays exponentially: $h[n] = 32\cdot(0.5)^n\cdot u[n] + 32\cdot(0.5)^{n-1}\cdot u[n-1]$, i.e., $h[0]=32,\ h[1]=48,\ h[2]=24,\ h[3]=12,\ldots$

2.2 ALC (FIR) identification model

The FIR ALC with $L+1$ coefficients:

$$y(k) = w^T u(k), \quad u(k) = [x(k),\,x(k-1),\,\ldots,\,x(k-L)]^T, \quad w = [w_0,\,w_1,\,\ldots,\,w_L]^T.$$ $$e(k) = d(k) - y(k), \quad e^2(k) = (d(k)-y(k))^2.$$

3. Wiener Solution (Correlation Method)

Minimising $J(w)=\mathbb{E}[e^2(k)]$ gives the Wiener–Hopf system:

$$R\,w_\text{opt} = P, \quad w_\text{opt} = R^{-1}P,$$ $$R = \mathbb{E}[u(k)u^T(k)], \quad P = \mathbb{E}[d(k)\,u(k)].$$

Sample estimates over $M$ valid samples:

$$\hat{R} = \frac{1}{M}U^TU, \qquad \hat{P} = \frac{1}{M}U^T\mathbf{d}.$$

Experiment settings

  • Samples: $N=8000$, seed: rng(1)
  • Input: $x(k)\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ (i.i.d. Gaussian white noise)
  • Initial conditions: $d(-1)=0$, $x(-1)=0$
  • Valid indices: $L=1\Rightarrow k=2,\ldots,8000\ (M=7999)$; $L=9\Rightarrow k=10,\ldots,8000\ (M=7991)$
  • $\operatorname{MSE}=\frac{1}{M}\sum_k e^2(k)$
CASE i — L+1 = 2

4. Results: $L+1=2$ (Two-tap FIR)

4.1 Estimated $R$, $P$, and Wiener weights $W$

$$R_{(L+1=2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1.01112902 & 0.01004599 \\ 0.01004599 & 1.01115053 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad P_{(L+1=2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 33.05383777 \\ 49.20388509 \end{bmatrix}.$$ $$W_{(L+1=2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 32.20973921 \\ 48.34127552 \end{bmatrix}.$$

The $R$ matrix is nearly diagonal (off-diagonal $\approx0.01$), reflecting the near-white-noise character of the i.i.d. Gaussian input. The two Wiener weights are close to $h[0]=32$ and $h[1]=48$, the first two samples of the true impulse response — but all higher-order contributions are lost.

$$\operatorname{MSE}_{(L+1=2)} = 787.3910068 \approx 7.87\times10^2.$$

4.2 Time-domain performance

$y(k)$ does not track $d(k)$ closely; $e^2(k)$ remains large throughout — consistent with the high MSE. The two-tap FIR cannot capture the exponential IIR tail, leaving a substantial modelling error at every sample.

CASE ii — L+1 = 10

5. Results: $L+1=10$ (Ten-tap FIR)

5.1 Estimated $R$, $P$, and Wiener weights $W$

$R_{(L+1=10)}$ is a $10\times10$ nearly-identity matrix (diagonal entries $\approx1.011$, off-diagonals $<0.013$), again reflecting the near-white input.

$$P_{(L+1=10)} = \begin{bmatrix} 33.0595 \\ 49.2157 \\ 25.3103 \\ 12.6767 \\ 6.2426 \\ 3.4847 \\ 1.7872 \\ 0.6296 \\ 0.5228 \\ 0.5672 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad W_{(L+1=10)} = \begin{bmatrix} 32.0006 \\ 48.0000 \\ 24.0012 \\ 12.0001 \\ 5.9997 \\ 3.0002 \\ 1.5008 \\ 0.7494 \\ 0.3758 \\ 0.1889 \end{bmatrix}.$$ $$\operatorname{MSE}_{(L+1=10)} = 0.01201656946 \approx 1.20\times10^{-2}.$$

5.2 Impulse-response interpretation of $W$

For $H(z)=(32+32z^{-1})/(1-0.5z^{-1})$, the true impulse response is $h[n]=32\cdot0.5^{n-1}(1+0.5^{n>0})\cdot u[n]$. The exact first-10 values and the identified weights match to within numerical noise:

Key insight: The identified FIR weights $W$ are a near-perfect truncation of the IIR impulse response $h[n]$. For an FIR model $\hat{H}(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{L}w_n z^{-n}$, the impulse response simply equals the coefficient vector: $\hat{h}[n]=w_n$. With $L+1=10$ taps the tail energy beyond $n=9$ is small ($h[10]=0.5^{10}\cdot48\approx0.047$), giving MSE $\approx0.012$.

5.3 Time-domain performance

$y(k)$ nearly overlaps $d(k)$; $e^2(k)$ stays close to zero with a 200-sample moving average at $\approx0.012$ — consistent with the very low MSE.

6. Frequency Response Comparison

Unknown IIR system on the unit circle:

$$H(e^{j\omega}) = \frac{32 + 32e^{-j\omega}}{1 - 0.5e^{-j\omega}}.$$

FIR model frequency response:

$$\hat{H}(e^{j\omega}) = \sum_{n=0}^{L} w_n\, e^{-j\omega n}.$$
  • $L+1=10$: closely matches $H(e^{j\omega})$ across almost the entire band — 10 coefficients capture a long portion of the exponentially decaying impulse response.
  • $L+1=2$: shows noticeable deviation, especially where the IIR "tail" contributes significantly (mid-to-high frequencies).

7. Discussion — Impulse Response Truncation

The unknown system $H(z)=(32+32z^{-1})/(1-0.5z^{-1})$ has an infinite-length impulse response that decays geometrically at rate $0.5$. For an FIR model $\hat{H}(z)=\sum_{n=0}^{L}w_n z^{-n}$, the impulse response equals its weight vector:

$$\hat{h}[n] = w_n, \quad n = 0,1,\ldots,L.$$

The Wiener solution therefore returns the optimal truncated approximation of $h[n]$. The truncation error energy is:

$$\varepsilon_\text{trunc}^2 = \sum_{n=L+1}^{\infty} h^2[n] = \sum_{n=L+1}^{\infty} \bigl(48\cdot 0.5^n\bigr)^2 = \frac{(48\cdot 0.5^{L+1})^2}{1-0.25}.$$

For $L+1=2$: $\varepsilon^2\approx 48^2\cdot0.25/0.75\approx768$, consistent with MSE$\approx787$. For $L+1=10$: $\varepsilon^2\approx48^2\cdot0.5^{20}/0.75\approx0.0044$, consistent with MSE$\approx0.012$.

8. Summary

Conclusion: For a white-noise input driving an IIR system, the Wiener FIR weights converge to a truncation of the true impulse response. Increasing $L+1$ from 2 to 10 reduces the MSE by a factor of $787.4/0.012 \approx 65{,}000\times$ ($\approx48\,\text{dB}$), because each additional tap captures one more sample of the decaying geometric tail $h[n]=48\cdot0.5^n$.
