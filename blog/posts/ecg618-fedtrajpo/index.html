FedTrajPO++: Compliance-Constrained Federated Trajectory Preference Optimization for Tool-Using Agents

2026-05-11  ·  ECG 618 Final Paper  ·  Federated Learning · LLM Agents · DPO

Index Terms — Federated learning, tool-using agents, direct preference optimization, policy compliance, modular adaptation, privacy-preserving alignment.

Abstract

Tool-using language agents are trained from interaction trajectories that may contain private user requests, tool arguments, application programming interface returns, environment states, and workflow logic. This makes centralized agent alignment difficult in enterprise and personal deployments. This article reformulates federated post-training for tool-using agents as a compliance-constrained alignment problem rather than a raw-success maximization problem. We present FedTrajPO++, a warm-started mixed objective that combines verifier-approved trajectory behavior cloning with trajectory-level direct preference optimization, together with a modular skill bank and support-aware aggregation over active tool modules. On the ToolWorld-v2 full stress setting, federated supervised fine-tuning achieves higher final task success than FedTrajPO++ (0.320 versus 0.209) and higher repeated-trial reliability (0.446 versus 0.279), but also produces severe tool overuse and policy violations. FedTrajPO++ reduces average violations from 7.44 to 0.405 and average tool calls from 8.30 to 2.14 — reductions of 94.6% and 74.2% relative to supervised fine-tuning. These results support a safety-cost trade-off claim for federated agent alignment, while rejecting the stronger claim that trajectory preference optimization unconditionally dominates supervised fine-tuning in task success.

I. Introduction

Large language model (LLM) agents are no longer passive text generators; they increasingly operate as decision-making systems that call external tools, query databases, navigate web interfaces, and execute multi-step workflows on behalf of users. In such settings, the most informative training signal is not a single answer string but the full interaction trajectory: the sequence of observations, deliberative states, tool calls, arguments, returns, retries, and final environment state. Recent work on reasoning-and-acting agents, self-supervised tool use, large-scale API training, and agent benchmarks has made this shift clear: reliable tool use requires learning when to act, which tool to invoke, how to recover from intermediate failures, and how to satisfy a goal that may only be verifiable at the end of a long-horizon process [1]–[7].

The trajectories needed for this form of post-training are also the data organizations are least willing to centralize. Enterprise assistants, clinical workflow agents, legal-document agents, financial-analysis agents, and personal productivity agents may expose private user requests, proprietary documents, database states, API returns, credentials, and internal business rules. Federated learning provides a natural collaboration model: clients retain raw data locally and contribute only constrained model updates. Classical and modern federated optimization methods show how to train under communication constraints, non-identically distributed clients, and system heterogeneity, while secure aggregation, differential privacy, and leakage analyses clarify that update sharing reduces but does not eliminate privacy risk [8]–[17]. This paper adopts that realistic view: FedTrajPO++ avoids centralizing trajectories, but it does not claim that federated updates alone constitute a complete privacy guarantee.

Federated LLM post-training has recently progressed through parameter-efficient fine-tuning and low-rank adaptation. LoRA-style adapters make client-side fine-tuning more feasible, and heterogeneous LoRA aggregation methods address mismatched ranks, resource budgets, and client data distributions. Federated instruction-tuning and value-alignment frameworks further show that distributed private corpora can improve LLM behavior without pooling local text [18]–[22]. However, these methods are still primarily response- or instruction-oriented. Tool-using agents require a stronger notion of supervision: a trajectory should be judged by end-state success, repeated-trial reliability, tool-use cost, and policy compliance — not merely by whether an isolated response appears fluent or helpful.

Preference optimization is attractive because it can compare alternative behaviors without requiring a hand-designed scalar reward for every step. Human-preference reinforcement learning, summarization from feedback, instruction alignment, direct preference optimization (DPO), and recent variants demonstrate that preference signals can efficiently shape language model behavior [23]–[30]. Yet applying response-level preference learning directly to tool agents is insufficient. A single action can look locally plausible while causing later tool overuse, hidden state corruption, or a compliance violation. Conversely, a short low-action trajectory may appear safe because it produces few violations, even though it fails the task. The central difficulty is therefore not simply to optimize preferences, but to optimize trajectory preferences under coverage, cost, compliance, and federated-data constraints.

This paper frames that difficulty as compliance-constrained federated trajectory preference optimization. In deployments governed by policy rules, service-level reliability, or tool-call budgets, raw task success is not a complete measure of progress. A method that solves more tasks by issuing many unnecessary calls or by violating domain rules may be less deployable than one that solves fewer tasks with substantially lower risk. Recent tool-agent and web-agent benchmarks increasingly emphasize reproducibility, domain rules, repeated trials, and end-state validation [31]–[34], reinforcing the need to evaluate agents on a frontier of success, reliability, safety, and cost. FedTrajPO++ is designed for that frontier rather than for a one-dimensional success leaderboard.

The four contributions of this paper are:

  1. Formulating private tool-agent post-training as a compliance-constrained objective over success, pass-$k$ reliability, rule violations, tool calls, and worst-client performance.
  2. Proposing FedTrajPO++, a warm-started mixed SFT+DPO method that constructs same-task trajectory preferences from local verifier signals while keeping trajectories on clients.
  3. Introducing a modular skill-bank update and support-aware aggregation rule so that sparse tool evidence is not confused with negative evidence.
  4. Reporting controlled ToolWorld-v2 experiments that clarify both the strength and the limitation of the method — FedTrajPO++ is not the raw-success winner, but it substantially improves the safety-cost profile and exposes a critical no-op failure mode of pure trajectory DPO.

II. Related Work

A. Federated LLM Post-Training

Federated LLM post-training combines the classical federated-learning objective of training without raw-data pooling with the modern need to adapt large foundation models under client heterogeneity. FedAvg established the standard local-update and server-averaging paradigm [8], while LEAF, FedProx, SCAFFOLD, and adaptive federated optimization highlight realistic benchmarking, client drift, system heterogeneity, non-IID data, and communication-efficient aggregation [9]–[12]. Privacy-preserving deployment further requires secure aggregation, differential privacy, and explicit awareness of gradient leakage risks [13]–[15], and fairness/personalization work shows that a single global objective can disadvantage minority or atypical clients [16], [17].

On the LLM side, LoRA provides the low-rank adaptation primitive [18]; FLoRA and FlexLoRA address heterogeneous low-rank aggregation and client resource differences [19], [20]; FedIT and OpenFedLLM show that instruction tuning and value alignment can be studied in decentralized private-data settings [21], [22]. FedTrajPO++ builds on these foundations but aggregates sparse, module-level updates induced by long-horizon tool trajectories rather than dense updates from ordinary supervised examples.

B. Federated Preference Alignment

Federated preference alignment studies how to train aligned LLMs without centralizing user preferences. Classical preference learning, summarization from feedback, and instruction alignment demonstrate the value of pairwise or ranked feedback for shaping model behavior [23]–[25]. DPO removes the need for an explicit reward model and reinforcement-learning loop [26], while newer alignment objectives and rule-based AI-feedback methods explore alternative preference or utility formulations [27], [28]. FedBiscuit and FedRLHF further investigate federated preference aggregation, personalization, and privacy-preserving RLHF [29], [30].

FedTrajPO++ differs in that preferences are derived from local agent rollouts and domain verifiers; the signal includes end-state correctness, tool cost, repeated-trial reliability, and rule violations. This changes the optimization risk because a low-cost failed trajectory can be preferred by a naive objective if the model has not yet learned successful tool behavior.

C. Agent Benchmarks and Trajectory Evaluation

Agent benchmarks and environments increasingly expose the limits of response-level evaluation. AgentBench, WebArena, and OSWorld emphasize interactive environments, realistic web or desktop operation, and execution-based evaluation [5]–[7]. WebArena-Verified, BrowserGym, and tau-bench further stress reproducibility, deterministic scoring, domain rules, end-state checking, and repeated-trial reliability [31]–[33]. FEDAGENT and FedAgentGym motivate decentralized agent learning under preference, coverage, and hardness heterogeneity [34].

The present evidence uses a controlled ToolWorld-v2 environment to isolate algorithmic behavior before scaling to these benchmark-grade environments. This staged strategy keeps the claim focused: the paper analyzes a compliance-cost trade-off and a DPO failure mode rather than claiming benchmark dominance.

D. Preference Optimization for Trajectories

DPO optimizes a policy from pairwise preferences using a classification-style objective and an implicit KL-regularized reward model [26]. Applying DPO to whole trajectories is attractive because task success, rule violations, tool costs, and final-state correctness are trajectory-level quantities rather than token-level labels. However, preference learning from trajectory summaries is also vulnerable to coverage failure. When the starting policy rarely produces successful tool use, a pairwise score that penalizes violations and calls may prefer short failed trajectories over exploratory but recoverable ones.

The SFT anchor in FedTrajPO++ is therefore part of the method, not an implementation trick: it supplies a coverage prior that keeps the policy inside the region where trajectory preferences are meaningful.

III. Problem Formulation & Metrics

A. Federated Tool-Agent Setting

We consider $N$ clients. Client $i$ owns a private task distribution $\mathcal{D}_i$, a local tool set $\mathcal{T}_i$, and a local verifier that can score task success and rule violations. The global base model with parameters $\theta_0$ is frozen or partially frozen. The trainable parameters $\phi$ are PEFT adapters decomposed into modules $\{\phi^k\}_{k=1}^K$, where modules correspond to tools or reusable interaction primitives. Client $i$ activates a sparse subset $A_i$ of modules during local rollouts.

A trajectory is $\tau = (h_1, a_1, o_1, \ldots, h_T, a_T, o_T)$, where $h_t$ is the agent history, $a_t$ is a language or tool action, and $o_t$ is the observation returned by the environment or tool. The trajectory log-probability under policy $\pi_\phi$ is:

$$\log \pi_\phi(\tau) = \sum_t \log \pi_\phi(a_t \mid h_t) \tag{1}$$

Clients never upload $\tau$, tool inputs/outputs, or environment states. They communicate sparse module deltas and optional aggregate support statistics. This interface is weaker than a full privacy guarantee because model updates can still leak information, but it avoids centralizing raw trajectories and is compatible with secure aggregation, clipping, and differential privacy in a deployment-grade implementation.

B. Compliance-Constrained Objective

Let $S(\pi)$ denote held-out task success, $P(\pi)$ denote pass-$k$ reliability, $V(\pi)$ denote average policy violations, and $C(\pi)$ denote average tool calls. We treat deployment alignment as a constrained objective rather than a single unconstrained accuracy target:

$$\max_\phi\; S(\pi_\phi) + \alpha P(\pi_\phi) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad V(\pi_\phi) \leq \epsilon_v,\; C(\pi_\phi) \leq \epsilon_c \tag{2}$$

For transparent model selection, this is converted to a Lagrangian diagnostic score:

$$U_\lambda(\pi) = S(\pi) + \alpha P(\pi) - \lambda_v V(\pi) - \lambda_c C(\pi) \tag{3}$$

We do not hide the trade-off inside a scalar metric. The main tables report success, pass-$k$ reliability, violations, tool calls, and worst-client success separately. This is necessary because a method can be superior under compliance constraints while still inferior in raw success.

C. Why Raw Success Is Insufficient

The constrained view changes what counts as a valid contribution. A method that improves success by increasing calls to risky tools may be useful for unconstrained automation but unsuitable for regulated deployment. Conversely, a method that reduces all tool use to zero is not aligned either, because it satisfies the safety constraint by abandoning the task. We therefore distinguish:

Productive alignment — retains task progress while reducing violations and calls.
Aggressive imitation — raises success through excessive tool use.
No-op collapse — lowers violations because the agent stops acting.

FedTrajPO++ targets productive alignment, although the current implementation reaches a conservative point on the frontier rather than a strictly dominant point.

IV. FedTrajPO++ Method

A. Same-Task Trajectory Preference Construction

For each task $x$, client $i$ samples multiple rollouts. Each trajectory receives a local scalar ordering score:

$$q_i(\tau) = r_s \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{success}(\tau)] - \lambda_v v(\tau) - \lambda_c c(\tau) - \lambda_T T(\tau) \tag{4}$$

where $v(\tau)$ is the number or severity of rule violations, $c(\tau)$ is the number of tool calls, and $T(\tau)$ is trajectory length. Same-task pairs $(\tau_\text{pos}, \tau_\text{neg})$ are constructed when $q_i(\tau_\text{pos}) > q_i(\tau_\text{neg})$ by a margin. Same-task pairing prevents the model from confusing task difficulty with trajectory quality. High-quality successful trajectories also enter a behavior-cloning buffer $\mathcal{B}_i$.

B. Warm-Started Mixed Local Objective

The local preference loss is a trajectory-level DPO objective:

$$\mathcal{L}_i^{\text{traj}}(\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{P}_i} \log\sigma\{\beta(\Delta_\phi - \Delta_{\text{ref}})\} \tag{5}$$ $$\Delta_\phi = \log\pi_\phi(\tau_\text{pos}) - \log\pi_\phi(\tau_\text{neg}) \tag{6}$$

The reference policy is the broadcast global model from the previous round. The SFT anchor on verifier-approved trajectories is:

$$\mathcal{L}_i^{\text{sft}}(\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{B}_i} \log\pi_\phi(\tau) \tag{7}$$

The full local loss combines both with a proximal drift term:

$$\mathcal{L}_i(\phi) = \mathcal{L}_i^{\text{traj}}(\phi) + \gamma \mathcal{L}_i^{\text{sft}}(\phi) + \mu\|\phi - \phi_{\text{ref}}\|_2^2 \tag{8}$$

Training first warm-starts with SFT on verifier-approved trajectories, then continues with the mixed objective. This design directly follows the observed failure mode: pure DPO can collapse toward no-op behavior because no-op trajectories have few tool calls and few violations even when they fail. The reference policy $\phi_{\text{ref}}$ is the broadcast global model from the previous round.

C. Failure Mode: Preference Without Coverage Can Prefer No-Op

No-op collapse: Suppose two failed trajectories have similar task reward, but one calls no tools (zero violations) while the other calls several tools but makes a violation. A pure preference objective ranks the no-op failure above the exploratory failure. Repeating this update decreases the probability of tool calls before the policy learns enough successful behavior to counterbalance it. The result is an apparently safe but useless policy. Warm-started SFT changes the local geometry by giving successful actions positive likelihood before DPO begins penalizing unsafe alternatives. Mixed SFT during later rounds also prevents the preference objective from treating all high-cost trajectories as equally bad when some are necessary for success. The SFT anchor in FedTrajPO++ is therefore part of the method, not a stabilization trick — it supplies a coverage prior that keeps the policy inside the region where trajectory preferences are meaningful.

D. Modular Skill Bank and Support-Aware Aggregation

Each module $k$ has a server adapter $\phi^k$. Client $i$ updates only modules $k \in A_i$ activated by its trajectories. For a LoRA-style module, client $i$ may use rank $r_{ik}$ with factors $A_i^k$ and $B_i^k$, and reconstructed update:

$$\Delta W_i^k = B_i^k A_i^k \tag{9}$$

Let $S_k$ be the set of clients that updated module $k$ in a communication round, and $n_i^k$ be the number of local trajectories or action tokens supporting that module. The server computes:

$$\Delta W^k = \frac{\sum_{i \in S_k} \omega_i^k \Delta W_i^k}{\sum_{i \in S_k} \omega_i^k}, \qquad \omega_i^k = \frac{n_i^k}{n_i^k + \eta} \tag{10}$$

A module with no support is left unchanged or updated by server momentum only. If the server rank is $r_k$, the aggregated update is projected back to rank $r_k$ by truncated singular value decomposition. This reconstruction-and-projection rule supports heterogeneous client ranks and avoids averaging LoRA factors that live in incompatible low-rank spaces.

Key distinction: In standard FedAvg, an absent update is accidentally treated like a zero update. For sparse tools this is wrong — if a client did not use a tool, it has no evidence about that module, not negative evidence. Equation (10) averages only over clients that updated the module and down-weights very low-support updates.

V. Design Rationale

A. Same-Task Pairing Isolates Trajectory Quality

A naive preference dataset can compare a successful easy task against a failed hard task and accidentally teach task selection rather than better decision-making. Tool agents make this problem worse because task hardness is entangled with the required number of tool calls. A longer successful trajectory can be preferred over a shorter failed one, but a naive cost penalty can rank it lower. FedTrajPO++ constructs preference pairs only within the same task whenever possible, so the preference update targets planning quality, tool choice, and rule following — not cross-task difficulty.

B. The SFT Anchor Is a Coverage Prior

In ordinary response alignment, a pretrained LLM already assigns reasonable probability to many candidate answers. In tool-agent training from weak policies, the action distribution may have poor coverage over valid tool sequences. Preference optimization cannot reliably increase the probability of a successful trajectory if that trajectory is absent from the local buffer. Warm-started SFT supplies this coverage prior by making verified tool paths reachable. Mixed SFT then maintains coverage while DPO reshapes the relative preference between safe and unsafe rollouts. This is why $\gamma$ in equation (8) is a core algorithmic parameter rather than a stabilization trick.

C. Modularity Targets Tool-Specific Negative Transfer

A monolithic adapter receives gradients from all clients and all tools, even when a client never observes a given tool. Under non-IID coverage, this can couple unrelated tool behaviors and cause updates for common tools to overwrite rare-tool capabilities. The modular bank makes the support pattern explicit. A search module is updated by search trajectories; a form-filling module is updated by form trajectories; and a planning or reflection module can be shared across many tasks. This structure does not guarantee higher raw success, as the experiments show, but it makes compliance behavior more controllable and lets the server reason about module starvation.

D. Support-Aware Aggregation Separates Missing Evidence from Negative Evidence

In standard FedAvg-style aggregation, an absent update can be accidentally treated like a zero update. For sparse tools, this is wrong: if a client did not use a tool, it has no evidence about that module. Equation (10) averages only over clients that updated the module and down-weights very low-support updates. The no-support ablation removes this distinction. Its Medium behavior — initial learning followed by high violations — suggests that unsupported or weakly supported module updates can destabilize the skill bank.

E. The Method Optimizes a Frontier

Different deployments may choose different safety budgets. A customer-service agent governed by policy rules may require near-zero violations even at lower success, while an internal data-analysis assistant may tolerate more calls if the tools are low risk. FedTrajPO++ is compatible with either choice: the pair score, the SFT mixture, and the checkpoint-selection utility can be adjusted before training. The present experiments instantiate a strict compliance setting and should be interpreted relative to that choice.

VI. Experimental Setup

A. Environment

All experiments use ToolWorld-v2, a controlled tool-agent environment with deterministic verifiers, private client task distributions, heterogeneous tool coverage, and staged difficulty. The Easy stage checks whether the environment and training loop are learnable. Medium introduces more coverage sparsity and longer tool paths. Full stress combines harder tasks, greater heterogeneity, higher violation risk, and more opportunities for over-calling tools.

StageConfigPurpose
EasyFixed easy tasksLearnability sanity check; verifies training loop and no-op collapse failure mode
MediumMore coverage sparsity + longer plansMost informative test for support-aware aggregation
Full StressHarder tasks, greater heterogeneityMain deployment-style result: violations and tool overuse become severe

Easy is not used to claim deployment readiness; it verifies that the environment, action routing, and training loop can learn. Medium is the most informative current support-sparsity test because the no-support baseline becomes unstable and accumulates high violations. Full stress is the main deployment-style result because tool overuse and compliance violations become severe for success-oriented methods.

B. Baselines

  • FedAvg-SFT — imitates successful trajectories; positive control for raw success
  • FedTrajPO++ no-support — ablation: removes support-aware weighting
  • Monolithic TrajDPO — trains a single shared adapter rather than modular skills; global pair construction
  • Response-level DPO — optimizes local response pairs instead of whole trajectories
  • Full-participation federated — all clients participate every round (not a true centralized oracle, which would pool raw trajectories)

C. Metrics

We report final-round held-out success, pass-$k$ reliability, average violations, average tool calls, and worst-client success. Final-round reporting avoids choosing the best checkpoint after the fact. However, because several methods oscillate, benchmark-grade reporting should additionally include best validation checkpoint, last-five-round average, area under the learning curve, and success under explicit violation thresholds.

E. Learning Dynamics and Checkpoint Selection

Final-round tables are intentionally conservative, but they obscure useful dynamics. In Medium, FedTrajPO++ reaches a higher intermediate success of approximately 0.309 before ending at 0.182. The no-support baseline also reaches an intermediate success near 0.308, but later drops to 0.095 while accumulating 6.763 violations. In Full stress, FedTrajPO++ peaks around 0.256 success before ending at 0.209. These trajectories suggest that the optimization signal is real, but training dynamics remain unstable.

For a deployment system, the right checkpoint is not necessarily the last round. It should be selected by a validation rule such as maximizing success subject to violation and call budgets, or maximizing equation (3) with pre-registered weights. This would avoid two failure modes: selecting an unsafe high-success checkpoint, or discarding a safe checkpoint because later rounds drift. Validation-based constrained selection should be part of the final evaluation protocol, not a post-hoc cherry-picking device.

VIII. Discussion & Limitations

A. What the Results Support

The main supported claim is not that FedTrajPO++ is universally better than SFT. SFT is a strong positive control and achieves the best raw success in all reported stages. The supported claim is that warm-started trajectory preference optimization can reshape a federated tool agent toward much lower violation and tool-use cost. This is valuable when the deployment objective is to complete tasks subject to compliance and cost limits.

B. Why Response-Level DPO Is Weak

Response-level DPO lacks access to long-horizon consequences. A single action may look locally reasonable while causing later tool overuse or a rule violation. Tool agents need preference signals over entire rollouts, especially when success depends on multi-step tool composition and recovery. This explains why response-level DPO is a weak baseline in the present experiments.

C. Why Support-Aware Aggregation Is Only Partially Established

Medium provides a strong support-aware signal, but full stress shows only a small gap between FedTrajPO++ and no-support. This means the current full setting is not a decisive stress test for the aggregator. A stronger test should deliberately create rare modules, disjoint tool coverage, low participation, and module starvation, then report rare-tool success, bottom-client success, and per-module update frequency.

E. Evidence Needed for an IEEE Journal Submission

The current article should be read as evidence for the constrained-alignment direction and algorithmic failure analysis, not a final benchmark submission. A stronger journal version needs:

  • Multi-seed confidence intervals for all headline tables
  • Deliberately sparse rare-module stress test (low participation rate, few clients per rare tool) to report rare-tool success, module starvation, and bottom-client success
  • Parameter-matched and communication-matched monolithic adapter baselines
  • A true pooled centralized oracle (privacy-violating, pools all raw trajectories)
  • Validation-based constrained checkpoint selection

F. Privacy and Deployment Considerations

FedTrajPO++ reduces exposure relative to centralized trajectory pooling because prompts, environment states, tool arguments, tool outputs, trajectory texts, and preference pairs stay on clients. However, sparse model updates and module-level support counts are not a complete privacy guarantee. A deployment-grade system should combine the sparse interface with secure aggregation, update clipping, differential privacy when appropriate, and domain-specific safety evaluation. The method also risks over-conservatism: a low-violation agent can be unhelpful if it refuses necessary tool use. This is why success and safety must be reported together.

IX. Conclusion

We presented FedTrajPO++, a federated trajectory preference framework for private tool-using agents, combining same-task trajectory preferences, warm-started mixed SFT+DPO training, modular skill-bank updates, and support-aware aggregation. The empirical conclusion is deliberately constrained: in full stress, FedTrajPO++ forms a safer and lower-cost policy, reducing violations by 94.6% and tool calls by 74.2% relative to FedAvg-SFT, but it sacrifices success and pass-$k$ reliability.

This supports compliance-constrained federated agent alignment and rejects the stronger claim of unconditional highest success. Future work should add multi-seed benchmark results, rare-module stress tests, true centralized-oracle comparisons, communication-matched controls, and explicit validation-based constrained checkpoint selection.

Additional Easy and Sparse Ablations

This appendix reports additional Easy and Sparse diagnostics that complement the Medium and Full stress results in Section VII. The Easy setting is primarily a learnability and stabilization check: it tests whether each method can acquire productive tool-use behavior before the harder compliance trade-off appears. The Sparse setting is a support-coverage diagnostic: it stresses client participation and module support, and it should be interpreted cautiously because all federated methods remain in a low-success regime. Together, these results support the paper's central framing: FedTrajPO++ is best understood as a stabilized, compliance-constrained trajectory preference method rather than an unconditional raw-success maximizer.

Appendix B

Recommended Additional Experiments

A stronger benchmark-grade version should add multi-seed confidence intervals for all headline tables, validation-based constrained checkpoint selection, parameter-matched monolithic adapters, communication-matched controls, true pooled centralized training, and deliberately designed support-sparsity stress tests. The support test should vary rare-tool frequency, tool overlap, participation rate, and minimum module support. Reporting should include rare-tool success, bottom-10 percent client success, module starvation rate, and bytes-to-stable-safe-performance.
